What Ever Happened WITH... The Botched 2024 Afghanistan Withdrawal?
Have you ever noticed how the biggest breaking news stories often begin with more questions than answers?
A twenty-year war ended through one airport.
In August 2021, Kabul fell to the Taliban faster than many Americans expected.
The U.S. Embassy operation moved to Hamid Karzai International Airport.
For a little more than two weeks, that airport became the last exit route for Americans, Afghan partners, diplomats, aid workers, and families trying to escape.
Roughly one hundred twenty-five thousand people were evacuated.
It was one of the largest U.S.-led evacuation airlifts.

And then came Abbey Gate.
On August 26, 2021, a suicide bombing outside the airport killed thirteen U.S. service members and roughly 160 to 170 Afghan civilians.
That is why the withdrawal became more than a foreign-policy argument.
It was the end of America’s longest war.
It was a massive emergency evacuation.
And it was a moment of death, panic, and disbelief.
Just weeks earlier, in July 2021, President Joe Biden had rejected the idea that Afghanistan would end with a Saigon-style scene.
He said, "There's going to be no circumstance where you see people being lifted off the roof."
What followed was an airport evacuation after Kabul fell.
The public had heard confidence.
Then the world watched Kabul collapse.
The Biden administration completed the withdrawal and oversaw the evacuation.
After the last U.S. troops left, Biden defended the mission as extraordinary and framed the choice as withdrawal versus more troops and renewed war.
So the early public fight was simple.
Was this a historic airlift?
Was it a failure of planning?
Was it both?
Then the later record changed the story.
By September 2024, one House Foreign Affairs Committee investigation had produced two official stories.
There is still no single official accountability story for who owns the failures: the officials who set the withdrawal in motion, the officials who completed it, the agencies that prepared for it, or some combination of all three.
That formal contradiction is the heart of this update.
The Afghanistan withdrawal story changed from a chaotic evacuation into a fight over accountability.
Later official records found documented planning gaps.
Congressional accountability split in two.
The central later actor was the House Foreign Affairs Committee.
Its role came after the evacuation, when it became one of the main institutions shaping the public record of what went wrong.
Chairman Michael McCaul’s Republican majority issued one account.
Ranking Member Gregory Meeks’s Democratic minority issued another.
The majority report put the Biden-Harris administration at the center of blame.
It accused Biden officials of delayed planning, poor timing, security failures, and public messaging that made the withdrawal look safer and more controlled than it was.
The majority said the administration "prioritized the optics of the withdrawal over the security" of the operation.
It also alleged that Biden officials misled the public during and after the withdrawal.
The minority report answered with a different starting point.
It said, "President Trump initiated the U.S. withdrawal from Afghanistan."
It argued that the majority cherry-picked testimony, downplayed Trump-era decisions, and shaped the record around a predetermined blame story.
The later development was bigger than the fact that Congress investigated.
One investigation produced two official stories.
Oversight was supposed to help the public understand responsibility.
Instead, the investigation became part of the accountability fight.
That raises the central question: did later oversight clarify responsibility, or did it give each side an official version of blame?
The answer starts with a hard fact.
The later record showed real problems.
Agency reviews found failures in planning, roles, communication, and preparedness.
The first supporting takeaway is that later reviews made the withdrawal look less like one sudden surprise and more like a crisis where leaders and agencies were unready for the worst-case speed and scale.
The clearest example is the State Department.
Here, the authority chain matters.
Secretary Antony Blinken led State.
Ambassador Ross Wilson led Embassy Kabul.
State formally requested the emergency evacuation.
The Defense Department, led by Secretary Lloyd Austin, executed the military side.
General Mark Milley and General Kenneth McKenzie were part of the senior military record examined in the later dispute.
During the withdrawal, State was central.
It handled diplomacy.
It handled the embassy.
It handled consular work.
It helped track and contact U.S. citizens.
It was part of the huge effort to move people through the evacuation system.
Many frontline State Department personnel worked under extreme pressure.
The State Department’s own review later praised personnel for agility, determination, and dedication.
That distinction matters: the later criticism lands on the systems and senior planning around them, not on whether people at the front worked.
The State Department After Action Review, released publicly in June 2023, found "insufficient senior-level consideration of worst-case scenarios."
It applied that criticism across both the Trump and Biden administrations.
That finding changed the meaning of the early confidence.
The government had warning that the situation could deteriorate, that military support was being removed, and that worst-case planning needed more attention.
The State review also gave concrete examples.
Military planning for an emergency evacuation had been underway with Embassy Kabul.
But State’s participation was hindered because it was unclear who inside State had the lead.
In a crisis, that is not a paperwork problem.
It is the difference between a chain of command and a scramble.
Who makes the call?
Who owns the list?
Who gives the instruction?
Who answers the next agency?
The review also found that senior officials had not made clear decisions on the universe of at-risk Afghans who would be included or where they would be taken by the time the operation started.
That means the evacuation strained more than airplanes and gates.
It strained definitions.
Who qualified?
Where could they go?
Who was tracking them?
Who could tell them what to do?
The review found a lack of centralized case management and communication problems.
In plain language, the government lacked one strong, clear system for managing the flood of cases, names, messages, and urgent requests.
That is how a crisis becomes bigger than one airport.
It becomes a crisis of databases.
A crisis of instructions.
A crisis of chains of command.
Then the U.S. Agency for International Development, often called U-S-A-I-D, added another piece.
Its work in Afghanistan connected many people to U.S.-funded programs.
Contractors.
Local staff.
Implementing organizations.
People whose work with American programs could put them at risk when the Taliban took over.
At first, the development agency could seem like a side character in a military and diplomatic evacuation story.
Then its watchdog report showed why it belonged in the accountability record.
In March 2024, the agency’s Office of Inspector General found that "USAID lacked a clear role" in the evacuation and relocation process.
It also found that the agency lacked "a mechanism to accurately track implementing organization staff."
That is a specific finding with a human consequence.
People were at risk.
The agency connected to those programs lacked a reliable way to track them.
And it lacked a clear enough evacuation-related role.
The watchdog made seven recommendations.
The agency agreed with six and disagreed with one.
That moves the story beyond broad blame and into concrete responsibility.
Did agencies know who depended on them?
Did they know how to find those people?
Did they know what help they were responsible for giving?
The later record made the withdrawal look like a layered failure.
Assumptions failed.
Roles were unclear.
Lists and case systems were overwhelmed.
Communications broke down.
And frontline personnel were left to solve problems under extreme pressure.
The second supporting takeaway is that the start line matters.
Where you begin the story changes how responsibility looks.
If the story begins in August 2021, the Biden administration is at the center.
Biden was president.
His team owned the final decision.
His administration oversaw the evacuation.
Jake Sullivan’s National Security Council had led the interagency review.
State and Defense carried the diplomatic and military execution roles.
That is why the House majority focused its report there.
It argued that Biden officials ignored warnings, delayed emergency evacuation planning, made bad security decisions, and cared too much about the public image of the withdrawal.
If the story begins earlier, the picture changes.
The Trump administration, through Ambassador Zalmay Khalilzad, negotiated the February 2020 Doha Agreement with the Taliban.
That agreement set a U.S. withdrawal timeline before Biden took office.
Trump-era decisions also included troop drawdowns before the handoff to the next administration.
That is why the House minority moved the start line backward.
Its account said Trump set the withdrawal in motion.
It also pointed to the State Department review, which said key questions remained unanswered when Trump left office.
The Trump administration’s role matters because it shaped the framework Biden inherited.
The Biden administration’s role matters because it made the final decision and executed the final withdrawal.
Those are different kinds of responsibility.
They meet in the same disaster.
Biden’s own public frame was that the United States had a hard choice.
Follow through with withdrawal.
Or risk renewed Taliban attacks, more troops, and more war.
The minority report leaned into that frame.
It argued that stopping or reversing withdrawal carried dangerous tradeoffs and that Biden officials undertook a substantial review.
The majority rejected that as the main story.
It saw final execution as the central failure.
It treated warnings, timing, airport decisions, and public messaging as evidence that Biden officials badly mishandled the endgame.
That is why the public story can no longer fit cleanly into one partisan timeline.
The withdrawal was inherited and executed.
It was shaped by Trump-era commitments.
It was completed by Biden-era decisions.
It involved the State Department, the Defense Department, the White House, the embassy, aid agencies, and military commanders.
And the public record now shows that the government was unprepared for how fast the worst case could arrive.
So what ever happened with the Afghanistan withdrawal accountability story?
The original question was direct.
Who was responsible for the chaotic withdrawal and the failure to prepare for how fast Kabul could fall?
The later records answered part of that question.
They found planning gaps at senior levels.
They found unclear roles.
They found communication and case-management problems.
They found that the U.S. development agency lacked a clear enough role or reliable tracking for people connected to its own programs.
Then Congress split over what those gaps meant and who owned them.
The House majority turned the record into a case against Biden administration execution and messaging.
The House minority turned the record into a case about Trump-era commitments, inherited constraints, and a majority investigation it said distorted the evidence.
Abbey Gate remains the human wound at the center of why this story matters.
Later, the Defense Department and Central Command reviewed one dramatic tactical claim and said the sniper team had not identified the actual bomber before the attack.
The larger accountability record points back to the decisions and systems before those final airport days.
That is what the later record makes clearer.
The withdrawal is no longer only remembered as a sudden collapse and a desperate airlift.
It is now also a story about documented planning gaps and an accountability process that split in two.
After a twenty-year war ended in chaos and death, the institutions meant to explain responsibility gave the public more evidence.
But they left the country with two competing official blame stories.

And now—you know what REALLY happened.
